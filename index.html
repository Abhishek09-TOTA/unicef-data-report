1.1 Top Best Performing Countries in Out-of-School Rate

FINDINGS & NEXT STEPS

The stark contrast between the best- and worst-performing countries highlights global education inequality. Countries with high enrollment rates benefit from strong government policies, stable economies, and robust schooling infrastructure. Meanwhile, nations with high out-of-school rates predominantly in Africa and South Asia struggle which can be due to factors such as poverty, gender-based barriers, and conflict. The gap is a reflection of educational policies and economic and social conditions that limit children's access to schooling. Addressing these disparities requires a multi-faceted approach, including investment in education, financial reforms, and targeted social interventions.

2. HOW HAVE THINGS CHANGED OVER TIME?

Progress in education is uneven, with some countries improving while others lag.

Which countries improved?
Some regions show a decline in out-of-school rates, suggesting the impact of policy reforms and investment in education.

Which countries stagnated or worsened?
Conflict zones and low-income countries struggle to make progress.

FINDINGS & NEXT STEPS

Tracking historical trends helps identify successful policies and regions where urgent action is needed. While some countries show improvements in school attendance, others remain stagnant or even worsen. Education reforms and policy interventions in certain regions have successfully reduced out-of-school rates but economic downturns, wars, and social upheavals have led to education crises in conflict zones. The time-series analysis is crucial for tracking progress and identifying regions where urgent action is needed. Governments and international organizations should replicate successful policies in struggling areas to ensure progress is sustained.

3. THE GLOBAL EDUCATION DIVIDE

Geographic disparities in school access are deeply rooted in economic inequality.
Many out-of-school children are concentrated in Africa, South Asia, and Latin America.
These regions often face poor infrastructure, gender barriers, and low government spending on education.

In many parts of Africa, South Asia, and Latin America, high out-of-school rates may be linked to factors such as inadequate infrastructure, conflict, and gender barriers. In contrast, Europe and East Asia tend to have stronger educational systems, potentially reflecting the impact of sustained investment in education. However, the variability within regions, especially in Africa and Asia indicates that while some countries are making progress, others may still require significant policy support. Additionally, the presence of outliers in typically well-performing regions, such as North America, suggests that pockets of inequality exist even in developed nations. These insights highlight the importance of considering region-specific interventions rather than a one-size-fits-all approach when addressing global education disparities.
